Punctuation Rules

 

Commas 
We use commas to:

 

separate three or more items in a list.

 

I need bananas, apples, eggs and milk.

 

separate two complete sentences joined by conjunctions and, but, or, yet.

 

I want to buy a new car, but I don’t have enough money.

 

Clarissa was swimming in the pool, and Pete was lying on the beach.

 

I can’t decide if I want to visit the zoo, or if I want to visit the museum.

 

separate a list of three or more verbs.

 

The students in the band march, play their instruments, and wave to the people.

 

separate parts of dates.

 

Monday, August 22 
nd
, 2017

 

Period 
We use periods to:

 

mark the end of a sentence.

 

Lucy went to the cinema with her friends yesterday evening.

 

After abbreviations:

 

Mr. / Mrs. / Ms. / Sun. / Jan. / A.M. / P.M. / B.C.

 

Question mark 
It is used after a direct question.

 

What do you want to do this weekend?

 

Exclamation  
It is used at the end of a sentence that expresses surprise, a strong feeling,  

 

mark 
or a command.

 

Be quiet!

 

Wow! What a surprise!

 

I can’t believe you are here!

 

Colon 
It introduces a list.

 

My friends are: Luisa, Ana, Paulina, and Mark.

 

It is also used before a long list of statements.

 

To be successful, you need: to work hard, to be disciplined, to be organized,  

 

to be honest.

 

Semicolon 
We use semicolon between two sentences in place of and, or, but.

 

Alan blocked the kick; Brian blocked the player.

 

When items in a list contain commas:

 

The shops on Busy Street belong to Mr. Jason, a chef; Mr. Marti, a butcher;  

 

and Ms. Sunnyside, a baker.

 

Quotation  
We use quotation marks to enclose a dialogue or direct conversation.

 

marks 
“You need to prepare the final presentation of the project,” said Mrs. Lauren.

 

Parentheses 
They are used to enclose related thought, additions, and explanations.

 

Mr. Stevenson (a very intelligent and cultivated person) has given us a talk about

 

modern art and its influence in young people. (Read the review in the web page.)

 

Ellipsis 
Is always three periods, no more, no less. We use ellipsis to show that words are

 

missing. We also use ellipsis to indicate a pause.

 

He looked at me for a second ... he didn't know what to say.

 

Brackets 
We use brackets before and after material that a writer adds when quoting

 

another writer.  
"Sometimes I think it [my writing] sounds like I walked out of a room

 

and left the typewriter running." 

 

We also enclose the Latin word  
sic
in brackets to indicate that an error that

 

appears in a quoted material was made in the original text.  
"No parent can

 

dessert [sic] his child ..."
