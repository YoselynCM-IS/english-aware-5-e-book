To write a biography ...

 

... first pick a person. 
There are many kinds of persons when writing a biography: dead people, celebrities

 

and relatives.

 

The research for each is very different. For dead people you have plenty of information in the library

 

and on Internet. Many people before you have already made a research and the challenge is finding

 

something new or putting together all the different kind of information. You will find out many people

 

repeat the same and some add new things. You can put them all together and decide what point of view

 

you will use to decide how to direct your biography.

 

Celebrities might still be alive and of course there will be a lot of fresh information on the Internet.

 

The challenge is to decipher which is true and which are lies. You have to be careful with the sources of

 

information you use.

 

Finally, relatives, they can be easy if they are still alive or persons who knew them, but old ancestors

 

might be more difficult to trace. You can always find information in older relatives or diaries. Finding out

 

about an ancestor might be very interesting and insightful.

 

... do your research. 
Look in the library, on the Internet or ask people who know or knew the person you

 

chose. Take notes. Write down everything, print everything, record everything.

 

... read your notes.  
Read everything once so you know what you found out. Read again to select repeated

 

information. Read again to discard useless or untrue information. Start ordering to make an outline.

 

... write an outline. 
Order you thoughts, your intentions and start structuring the biography you are

 

going to write. Divide the biography in ...

 

Introduction  
where you will write a short summary stating the dates of birth and death, place of birth,

 

mother and father. Explain why that person is important, what he / she did.

 

Development  
where you start by writing where he / she studied, worked or started becoming that

 

interesting person. This can take more than one paragraph. You can read the biographies as examples.

 

Conclusion 
where you state prizes or the legacy of the person. The legacy means what the person left,

 

not only what he / she did, but the difference he / she made in history. Dolores del Río opened the path

 

to Mexican actresses; Leonardo Da Vinci made a lot of studies and he also invented things we still use

 

today; Sandra Cisneros won prizes and opened a shop to teach others like her and Octavio Paz won the

 

Nobel Prize. A relative might have been famous, but the most important thing, is he / she was part of a

 

chain of events that brought you to life!

 

... write the biography following your outline.  
Be careful with order, grammar, spelling and punctuation.

 

You tell a biography in third person, that is, you write about him or her. If you have doubts, consult a

 

manual of grammar and a dictionary. Stop and reread several times what you are reading to see if it

 

makes sense and to see you don’t forget anything. Give it a rest and reread. Give it to someone else to

 

check if it is clear. Listen to others and make corrections.

 

... print it.  
Once you are sure the biography has everything you wanted it to say, you can print it.
